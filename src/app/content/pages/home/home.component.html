• "Angkor Bayon Temple"

    Angkor is one of the most important archaeological sites in South-East Asia. Stretching over some 400 km2, including forested area, Angkor Archaeological Park contains the magnificent remains of the different capitals of the Khmer Empire, from the 9th to the 15th century. They include the famous Temple of Angkor Wat and, at Angkor Thom, the Bayon Temple with its countless sculptural decorations. UNESCO has set up a wide-ranging programme to safeguard this symbolic site and its surroundings.

  • "Bagan Temples"

    This heritage site, with its intricate designs and serene atmosphere, invites visitors to step back in time and immerse themselves in the spiritual and historical significance of the place. Each structure tells a story of a civilization that valued art, religion, and community, leaving behind a legacy that has withstood the test of time.

  • "Mughal architecture in India"

    The palace is not just a historical monument but also a cultural hub, housing museums that showcase an extensive collection of royal artifacts, including costumes, weapons, and manuscripts. Visitors are transported back in time, experiencing the opulence and splendor that once defined the lives of Rajasthan's royalty.

  • "Gangaikonda Cholapuram Temple"

    The Gangaikonda Cholapuram Temple, located in Tamil Nadu, is a monumental example of the architectural brilliance and cultural grandeur of the Chola dynasty. Constructed in the 11th century by Rajendra Chola I, this temple was designed to surpass the magnificence of the Brihadeeswarar Temple in Thanjavur, reflecting the Cholas' wealth, power, and devotion to Lord Shiva.

  • "Mamallapuram Shore Temple"

    The Shore Temple in Mamallapuram, also known as Mahabalipuram, is one of the most iconic and ancient architectural marvels in India. Located on the shores of the Bay of Bengal in Tamil Nadu, this temple is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and is renowned for its historical, cultural, and architectural significance.

  • "Humayun's Tomb"

    Humayun's Tomb is a magnificent example of Mughal architecture in India and was built in the mid-16th century. Commissioned by Humayun's widow, Empress Bega Begum, the tomb is known for its grand scale, symmetrical gardens, and use of red sandstone with white marble inlay. It inspired the later construction of the Taj Mahal and represents a blend of Persian, Turkish, and Indian architectural styles. The tomb is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and serves as a significant cultural and historical monument in India.

Preserving India's Rich Heritage

Government Initiatives and Cultural Revival

India, with its vast history and cultural diversity, is home to numerous ancient sites and heritage monuments that are of immense significance. The government of India has acknowledged the importance of preserving the nation’s timeless and rich cultural heritage. The government’s effort has been led from the front by Prime Minister Shri Narendra Modi under the slogan he gave, ‘Vikas Bhi Virasat Bhi’. The Prime Minister has accorded pivotal importance to protecting and promoting Indian knowledge systems, traditions, and cultural ethos, both nationally and internationally. One of the notable accomplishments is the redevelopment of neglected sites of civilizational significance. As of May 2023, a total number of 45 projects at a cost of Rs 1584.42 crore covering pilgrimage sites across the country have been approved under PRASAD (Pilgrimage Rejuvenation and Spiritual Augmentation Drive) scheme by the Government in its unwavering commitment to safeguarding India's ancient civilizational heritage.

Reviving cultural heritage is not a solitary effort; it requires the involvement of the entire community. We collaborate with local artisans, elders, and cultural institutions to ensure that the knowledge passed down through generations is not lost. By engaging with these stakeholders, we create a living connection to our heritage that resonates with all members of the community.Our long-term goal is to ensure that our cultural heritage is not just preserved but celebrated. By reviving these traditions, we hope to foster a sense of pride in our shared identity, strengthen community bonds, and create opportunities for cultural tourism that will sustain these practices for future generations.

How India can save its urban heritage

“When Vikas Dilawari started his practice in 1989, architectural conservation was a relatively unknown occupation in India. The first project that came to him, a few years later in 1993, was from a Parsi trust that commissioned the restoration of the Byramjee Jejeebhoy Parsi Charitable Institution in the Charni Road area of Mumbai. Besides these private entities, there were few supporting the conservation of heritage structures at the time. There were no guidelines in place either, besides what Dilawari had grasped while pursuing a masters degree in conservation at the School of Planning and Architecture in New Delhi and then at the University of York. That first project is something that his team maintains even today, even as the field itself has made rapid progress, which in turn has helped such heritage structures survive amid the pressures of urban development.”

While Mumbai took the lead on heritage conservation, in places like New Delhi it took until 2010 to have any such regulations in place. Monuments such as Humayun’s Tomb and the Qutub Minar drew their fair share of attention from the ASI, but smaller structures and private residences went unattended. Architect Aishwarya Tipnis considers it to be a problem of plenty.

Wat Chedi Luang

Known as the Temple of the Great Stupa, Wat Chedi Luang is among the most famous temples in Chiang Mai. It is most symbolically identified by its incomplete pagoda, which was brought down from a lofty 85 meters to 60 meters after an earthquake in 1545 and now remains partly destroyed. Nevertheless, it is an enormous structure engraved with mythological motifs and enshrining several Buddha images. A replica of the revered Emerald Buddha of Wat Phra Kaew rests inside the temple, and the compound also houses the sacred city pillar, making it a highly venerated religious site.
